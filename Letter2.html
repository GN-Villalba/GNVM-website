Letter 2: To Young Researchers Who Are Not Always Orange

On comparison, visibility, and the colours we rarely show

Written after spending too much time on LinkedIn

This afternoon, I opened LinkedIn while waiting for a dataset to load. It was supposed to be a short break, the kind of harmless pause we convince ourselves we deserve before returning to work. Instead, it became one of those moments that leaves you questioning things you were not questioning ten minutes earlier.

There was post after post about good news: someone had been accepted to a conference, someone else had received a fellowship, another person was celebrating a new publication. As I kept scrolling, I found myself comparing my own progress to theirs. Even though I was happy for them, I started to feel as if I was falling behind.

For a while, I thought the problem was social media itself. We often say that these platforms only show the highlights, and that is true. But today I realised that what exhausted me was not seeing other people shine. It was the quiet feeling that I should always be shining too.

What exhausted me was not seeing other people shine. It was the quiet feeling that I should always be shining too.

Most people know a particular version of me. They know the one who presents her research with enthusiasm, who teaches, who shares achievements, who smiles in photographs, who speaks about future plans and ongoing projects. That person is real. I enjoy being her. She is not a performance or an invention. If I were a colour, she would probably be orange: warm, energetic, hopeful, impossible to miss.

But there are other colours too.

There are the days when opening a dataset feels intimidating because I am afraid of what I might find. The evenings spent worrying about delayed income, unfinished papers, or whether I am making enough progress. The moments of doubt that appear without warning, even after years of work. The quiet exhaustion that accumulates when every project matters deeply. Those colours are real too. Some days they are violet. Occasionally, they are grey.

I do not mind having those days. What I mind is feeling as though I should hide them. As though uncertainty must always be edited out of the story. As though vulnerability needs to be transformed into a lesson before it can be shared. As though being a successful young researcher means remaining permanently bright, motivated, grateful, and composed.

Perhaps this is why visibility can feel so heavy. Not because people are dishonest, but because we are all participating in the same unspoken agreement. We show the acceptance, not the rejection. The conference, not the loneliness of travelling alone. The finished paper, not the months spent staring at a stubborn paragraph or rerunning the same code. The celebration, not the cost of getting there.

After a while, we forget that everyone else is carrying invisible colours too.

After a while, we forget that everyone else is carrying invisible colours too.

And so I wanted to write this letter, not only to those who feel overwhelmed by what they see online, but also to those who seem to have everything figured out. Because I suspect that many of us are far more alike than we imagine. The colleague celebrating a publication may be worrying about funding. The student presenting brilliant results may be questioning their abilities. The researcher smiling in a conference photograph may have spent the previous week feeling completely lost.

We rarely see those moments, but that does not mean they do not exist.

If you are reading this after a difficult day, I hope you remember something I am trying to remind myself of: you do not lose your place in academia on the days when you feel uncertain. You do not become less capable because you are tired. You do not need to be orange all the time.

There is room here for the violet days too.

There is room here for the violet days too.

And perhaps there is something deeply human about allowing ourselves to be seen in more than one colour.
