Letter 1: To those of us who teach while doing a PhD

Reflections before the autumn semester begins

Written somewhere between London and Falmer

This autumn I’ll be supporting an Econometrics course, and although the semester hasn’t even started yet, I already feel something stirring. A quiet excitement. A sense of purpose. And, of course, the early signs of a spreadsheet-induced identity crisis.

Let’s be honest: teaching while doing a PhD is a lot. I regularly get lost in Stata outputs and forget how to talk like a human being. But somehow, the idea of walking into a classroom, being present, explaining, interacting, grounds me. Teaching pulls me out of the PhD tunnel and reminds me there’s a world beyond drafts and deadlines.

It also reminds me of why I’m here in the first place.

I still remember the first time I entered a classroom as a teaching assistant. I had just finished my undergraduate degree and was holding a whiteboard marker like it was a sword I didn’t quite know how to use. I was excited, yes. But I also felt overwhelmed and unsure of myself. A part of me didn’t feel enough. I wanted to help my students and add real value, but I doubted I could.

That feeling stayed with me. And it became one of the deepest reasons why I kept studying. I didn’t just want more degrees. I wanted to become the kind of teacher who’s actually ready — present, solid, helpful. The kind of teacher I wish I had when I was on the other side of the classroom.

That’s why I did a master’s. That’s why I’m doing a PhD. And sometimes, when research feels too heavy or abstract, teaching reminds me why I’m still here. It brings me back to what really matters. It keeps me going.

Now, as I prepare to support Econometrics, a subject that has never scared me but has always challenged and excited me, I’m reminded of why I fell in love with it in the first place. Yes, it’s hard. Historically intimidating for many. But maybe that’s exactly why I’ve always been drawn to it.

It doesn’t flatter you. It tests you.

It demands clarity, structure, precision. And I find something beautiful in that. I don’t approach it with fear, but with respect. And with the hope that I can help others see what I see in it — not a wall, but a door.

So if you're preparing for your first class, wondering if you're qualified, or thinking about how to balance teaching with, well, everything else — I see you. I feel the chaos too. And I want you to know:

You don’t need to be perfect to make a difference. Your presence matters. Even when you doubt it. Especially then.

— gNoemi V.
