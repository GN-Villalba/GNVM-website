About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Economics at the University of Sussex, specialising in Applied Microeconomics, with a focus on the economics of education, labour markets, and development.

My research explores how public policies affect educational quality and labour market outcomes, especially in contexts marked by inequality. I use causal inference methods such as Instrumental Variables (IV), Difference-in-Differences (DiD), and Event Studies to understand how systems shape — and often limit — human potential.

I work mainly with data from Latin America, but my questions are global: What makes a good education? Who gets access to opportunity? How do we measure change, and who gets to define it?

Beyond academic metrics, I’m drawn to the quieter sides of economics: gender, social mobility, and the invisible scaffolding that shapes who gets a chance. But I also follow my curiosity into sports, fashion, and other spaces that might not scream “economics” but are full of meaning. These are not just interests; they’re questions worth researching about innovation, value, and how people create identity through markets often overlooked by mainstream analysis.

This website is more than a CV. It’s a place where I share research, teaching, reflections, and — occasionally — poetry. Because I believe we can be rigorous and human at once.
